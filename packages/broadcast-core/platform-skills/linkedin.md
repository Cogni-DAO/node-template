# LinkedIn — Platform Skill Guide

## Platform Identity

LinkedIn is a professional networking platform where content is tied to personal/company reputation. The algorithm heavily favors native text posts over links. Content that generates thoughtful comments ranks highest. The audience is professional — they're evaluating you as a colleague, hire, or partner.

**Primary audience:** Professionals, founders, engineers, investors, recruiters. B2B decision-makers.
**Content lifespan:** 24-72 hours (longer than X, shorter than blog). Top posts resurface for weeks.
**How content spreads:** Algorithmic feed weighted by: comments > reactions > shares. Network connections see engagement. 2nd-degree reach is massive.

## Format Constraints

- **Max length:** 3000 characters (posts), 1300 characters visible before "see more"
- **Media:** Single image, carousel (PDF upload), video (native > link), documents
- **Links:** Algorithm deprioritizes posts with external links. Put link in first comment instead.
- **Hashtags:** 3-5 relevant ones. LinkedIn uses them for topic routing.
- **Formatting:** Line breaks, emojis as bullets, bold (not supported natively — use caps or emojis for emphasis)
- **No markdown:** Plain text only. Use line breaks and spacing for structure.

## What We Optimize For

| Goal                   | Weight  | How                                                               |
| ---------------------- | ------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Comments (thoughtful)  | Highest | End with a genuine question. Share a decision, not just a result. |
| Professional authority | High    | Show expertise through specifics, not claims                      |
| Reach / impressions    | Medium  | First 1300 chars must hook — determines "see more" clicks         |
| Link clicks            | Low     | Links go in comments, not post body                               |

## Tone & Voice

**What works:**

- First-person narratives ("I spent 6 months building X. Here's what I'd do differently.")
- Lessons learned with specific numbers/outcomes
- Contrarian professional takes backed by experience
- "Building in public" updates with substance
- Acknowledging failure or difficulty (vulnerability + insight)

**What fails:**

- "We're hiring!" as the entire post
- Humble brags disguised as gratitude ("Humbled to announce...")
- Inspirational quotes without original thought
- Engagement bait ("Agree? Comment below!")
- Reposting press releases verbatim
- Excessive emojis or LinkedIn-bro formatting (every. single. line. is. one. word.)

## Content Patterns

### Thought Leadership

```
[Hook: contrarian statement or surprising result]

[2-3 paragraphs of context and insight]

[Specific data/outcome]

[Question that invites professional perspectives]

#relevanthashag #industry #topic
```

### Project Update

```
[What we shipped and why it matters — 2 sentences]

The problem:
[What was broken / what users struggled with]

What we built:
[3-4 bullets with specifics]

What surprised us:
[1-2 genuine insights from the process]

What would you have done differently?

[Link in comments]
```

### Hiring / Team

```
[Why this role exists — the mission, not the JD]

What you'd actually work on:
- [Specific project 1]
- [Specific project 2]

What makes this different:
- [Genuine differentiator]

DM me or link in comments.
```

## Adaptation Rules

When adapting content for LinkedIn:

1. **Hook in the first 2 lines.** Everything after ~150 chars is behind "see more."
2. **No links in the post body.** Put them in the first comment. The algorithm punishes external links.
3. **Write in first person.** "We" is okay for company updates, but "I" performs better.
4. **End with a real question.** Not "thoughts?" — a specific question that invites professional perspective.
5. **Use line breaks aggressively.** LinkedIn has no markdown — visual structure comes from whitespace.
6. **3-5 hashtags at the bottom.** Industry terms, not branded hashtags.
7. **Specifics over generalities.** "Reduced deploy time from 45min to 3min" beats "significantly improved deployment speed."

## Examples

### Strong

> We put our entire billing system on a blockchain.
>
> Not because it's trendy. Because we have 47 DAO members who need to independently verify every transaction — and none of them should have to trust a central database.
>
> What we learned after 6 months in production:
>
> Settlement time went from "whenever finance gets to it" to 24h automatic.
> Dispute resolution dropped 90% — the ledger is the truth.
> Gas costs are real — we spend ~$40/mo on L2 transactions.
>
> The biggest surprise? The hardest part wasn't the tech. It was getting 47 people to agree on the accounting categories.
>
> For anyone considering on-chain accounting for a DAO: the transparency is worth it, but budget 3x your estimate for governance decisions.
>
> What's your experience with transparent financial systems in distributed orgs?
>
> #DAO #blockchain #fintech #accounting #web3
